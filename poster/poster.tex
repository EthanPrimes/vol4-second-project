\begin{center}
\LARGE{\textbf{The Zermelo Navigation Problem}}
\end{center}

We begin by solving the standard Zermelo Navigation problem, where we are piloting a motor boat through water with a current and want to travel between two points as fast as possible. We chose $\theta,$ the angle between the $x$-axis and our heading, to be our control, and assumed that the velocity of the boat would be given by
\[
\begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \end{bmatrix} = v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix},
\]
This yields the following velocity:
\[
\begin{bmatrix} \dot x \\ \dot y \end{bmatrix} = v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} w_1(x, y) \\ w_2(x, y) \end{bmatrix},
\]
where $\mathbf{w}$ refers to the impact of current.

So, we are trying to minimize the functional

\begin{align*}
    J[u] &= \int_{t_0}^{t_f} 1 dt\\
    \text{subject to } \dot{\vec{s}} &= \vec{u}(t) + \vec{w}(\vec{s}(t))\\
    \vec{s}(t_0) &= \vec{s}_0\\
    \vec{s}(t_f) &= \vec{s}_f \\ \| u \| = v \cos(\theta - \phi)
\end{align*}
So the lagrangian constraint version is 

$$ \tilde{J}[u] = \int_{t_0}^{t_f} 1 + \lambda^T (\dot{\vec{s}} - \vec{u}(t) - \vec{w}(\vec{s}(t))) dt $$

And so


\begin{align*}
H &= \lambda^\top f - L \\
  &= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & \lambda_2 \end{bmatrix}
     \left( v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta - \phi) \cos(\theta) + w_1(x, y) \\ \cos(\theta - \phi) \sin(\theta) + w_2(x, y) \end{bmatrix} \right) - 1
\end{align*}


By Pontryagin's Maximization principle, we have


\begin{align*}
\dot{x} &= v\cos(\theta - \phi) \cos(\theta) + w_1(x, y) \\
\dot{y} &= v\cos(\theta - \phi) \sin(\theta) + w_2(x, y) \\
\dot{\lambda_1} &= -\lambda_1 \frac{\partial w_1}{\partial x} -\lambda_2 \frac{\partial w_2}{\partial x} \\
\dot{\lambda_2} &= -\lambda_1 \frac{\partial w_1}{\partial y} -\lambda_2 \frac{\partial w_2}{\partial y} \\
H(t_f) &= 0
\end{align*}


Where the last equality comes from the fact that we are maximizing over unknown time. We get that $\frac{\partial H}{\partial u} = 0$ as well.

Since we are maximizing $H$, and the only term where $u$ shows up is $\lambda \cdot u$, we can just maximize that to solve for u.

We get $u = \frac{\lambda}{\| \lambda \|} v(\theta)$ at each time $t$, which we can plug into the above system.

\LARGE{\textbf{The Simplest Approach}}
