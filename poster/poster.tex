\begin{center}
\LARGE{\textbf{The Zermelo Navigation Problem}}
\end{center}

We begin by solving the standard Zermelo Navigation problem, where we are piloting a motor boat through water with a current and want to travel between two points as fast as possible. We chose $\theta,$ the angle between the $x$-axis and our heading, to be our control, and assumed that the velocity of the boat due to the motor would be given by
\[
\begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \end{bmatrix} = v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix},
\]
Accounting for current yields the following velocity:
\[
\begin{bmatrix} \dot x \\ \dot y \end{bmatrix} = v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} w_1(x, y) \\ w_2(x, y) \end{bmatrix},
\]
where $w_1$ and $w_2$ refer to the impact of current. So, we are trying to minimize the functional

\begin{align*}
    J[u] &= \int_{t_0}^{t_f} 1 dt\\
    \text{subject to } \dot{\mathbf{s}} &= \mathbf{u}(t) + \mathbf{w}(\mathbf{s}(t))\\
    \mathbf{s}(t_0) &= \mathbf{s}_0\\
    \mathbf{s}(t_f) &= \mathbf{s}_f \\ \| u \| = v \cos(\theta - \phi)
\end{align*}
So the Lagrangian constraint version will be given by
\[
\tilde{J}[u] = \int_{t_0}^{t_f} 1 + \lambda^T (\dot{\mathbf{s}} - \mathbf{u}(t) - \mathbf{w}(\mathbf{s}(t))) dt,
\]
and the Hamiltonian will be given by
\begin{align*}
H &= \lambda^\top f - L \\
  &= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & \lambda_2 \end{bmatrix}
     \left( v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) + w_1(x, y) \\ \sin(\theta) + w_2(x, y) \end{bmatrix} \right) - 1
\end{align*}
By Pontryagin's Maximization principle, an optimal solution must satisfy
\begin{align*}
\dot{x} &= v \cos(\theta) + w_1(x, y) \\
\dot{y} &= v \sin(\theta) + w_2(x, y) \\
\dot{\lambda_1} &= -\lambda_1 \frac{\partial w_1}{\partial x} -\lambda_2 \frac{\partial w_2}{\partial x} \\
\dot{\lambda_2} &= -\lambda_1 \frac{\partial w_1}{\partial y} -\lambda_2 \frac{\partial w_2}{\partial y} \\
H(t_f) &= 0
\end{align*}
Where the last equality comes from the fact that we are maximizing over unknown time. We get that $\frac{\partial H}{\partial u} = 0$ as well.

\par Since we are maximizing $H$, and the only term where $u$ shows up is $\lambda \cdot u$, we can just maximize that to solve for u.

We get $u = \frac{\lambda}{\| \lambda \|} v(\theta)$ at each time $t$, which we can plug into the above system.

\begin{center}
\LARGE{\textbf{The Simplest Sailing Approach}}
\end{center}
\large

But what if we are sailing using the wind and currents instead of a motor? This model makes the assumption that current and wind are the only factors we need to account for, and that they directly control velocity. More precisely, the impact of wind on the boat's velocity is

\[
\begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \end{bmatrix} = v \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta - \phi) \cos(\theta) \\ \cos(\theta - \phi) \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix} 
\]
where the new $\cos(\theta - \phi)$ term determines the exposure of the sail to the wind. This makes the boat go at full speed when it's facing the same way as the wind, and is still when its sail is orthogonal to the wind. The derivation of the solution is nearly identical to the previous one.

\begin{center}
\vspace{2cm}
\LARGE{\textbf{Physically Accurate Sailing}}
\end{center}
\large However, wind doesn't move boats by ``blowing on the sails;'' For boats with triangular sails, it's more accurate to say that sails act like an airplane wing, creating thrust as wind moves faster over their front than their back. In fact, boats rarely sail directly downwind since this makes them very unstable! A more accurate model would have boats going fastest about 70$^\circ$ off of straight downwind, called a "Broad Reach". Not only does this allow sailboats with triangular sails to sail \textit{across} the wind, or orthogonally to the wind, it also allows the sailboat to sail about 40$^\circ$ from directly upwind! So sailboats can effectively move in any direction, in a straight line for friendly directions, and in zig-zags if close to directly up-wind or directly down-wind. Switching which zig you are zagging while going upwind is called \textbf{tacking}. Switching while going downwind is called \textbf{jibing}. One main objective of this project is to \textit{discover} tacking and jibing as part of the optimal solution.

\FancySubtitle[4mm][1cm]{Conclusions and next steps}

\large 

\large We have a lot of further directions we want to explore. The first things that we want to incorporate into our models are land penalties, to prevent the boats from running into land or islands. This is the most obvious issue with the optimal solutions we have computed so far.

\large Similarly, it is not physically realistic that the wind directly controls the boat's velocity. Instead, the wind should impact the boat's \textit{acceleration}. Solving this optimal control problem will likely yield even more accurate results, although it will take more effort to implement.
